RESEARCH PAPERS

  • Family Income and Schooling relationship in the US and its evolution (ongoing)

Investment in human capital is an important source of productivity growth. However, credit constraints can distort skill investment decisions and result in less than socially optimal educational attainment. If borrowing constraints prevent economically disadvantaged youth from attending post-secondary institutions, the outcomes will be inefficient. However, if the reason for low-income youth not attending university would be that they’re not well-qualified or they don’t have taste for school, then the existing education gaps by income are not necessarily inefficient. That’s the reason for studying the underlying causes for family income – schooling attainment gaps. Previous research has generally shown that the effect of relative family income (interquartile) on educational attainment has increased over the years. However, in a recent paper, Belzil and Hansen (2020) show that the effect of real family income, as opposed to relative family income, on educational attainment has decreased between the early 1980s and early 2000s using a dynamic model and data from NLSY79 and NLSY97. This paper is an extension of Belzil and Hansen's work and studies the effect of real family income, cognitive and non-cognitive ability as well as the number of hours worked for pay during school days on the educational attainment of two cohorts of youth (NELS:88 and HSLS:09).

  • Effect of Participation in After-School Activities and Working While in School on High School Performance in the US (ongoing)

In this paper, I use data from two American surveys (NELS:88 and HSLS:09) to study the effect of participation in different activities during the school year and the intensity of the participation, on academic performance. Similar to Hansen (2008) and unlike most of the previous literature, which has focused mainly on student employment, this paper also considers extracurricular activities. The paper utilizes longitudinal data to take into account the sequential nature of students’ decisions and outcomes. Data from NELS88 shows that in 1990 and 1992 in the US, more than 80 percent of students mostly in 10th and 12th grade respectively, participated in some form of extracurricular activity. This proportion for the 2009 cohort, was more than 90 percent for 9th graders and around 80 percent for students mostly in 11th grade. Since it is likely that participating in extracurricular activities and working while in school are chosen endogenously, different methods have been used to study the impact of participation on academic outcomes. For working while in school, the results show that around 30 years ago, in 1990, working while in 10th grade did not have any significant impact on academic performance and the result is the same for 1992 when most of the cohort was in 12th grade. Also, work participation during 10th grade did not have any significant adverse effects on the GPAs in 12th grade in 1992. Around 20 years later, in 2009, participation in the workforce still didn’t have any significant effects on academic performance, while working in 2009 adversely and significantly affects the grade point averages of students in 2011. Participation in extracurricular activities in all these years had a positive significant effect on academic performance.

  • Estimating the Technology of Youth Cognitive and Noncognitive Skill Formation (ongoing)

There exists a wide dispersion in measured human capital in children and young adults. Since human capital has a strong correlation with later life outcomes such as lifetime income and the highest level of education completed, it is important to understand the determinants of skills formation among these individuals. However, the empirical challenges in estimating the skill formation process (in other words, the technology of child development), are caused by the likely imperfect measures of children's skills available. In this paper, I study the process of young teenagers' (14-18 years old) cognitive and noncognitive skill formation using a dynamic latent factor structure, and data from an American survey (NELS:88). The identification of the skill formation process is challenging because these skills are only observed through arbitrarily scaled and imperfect measures. I am using the identification method presented by Agostinelli and Wiswall (2020) with the constraint on the production function and their sequential estimation algorithm for the joint dynamic process of latent investment and skill development.
